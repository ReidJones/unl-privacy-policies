Background on Student Privacy

In February 1969, the Supreme court ruled that students do not “shed their constitutional rights to freedom of speech or expression at the schoolhouse gate.” Since then, several pieces of legislation have been passed and several Supreme Court cases have been decided that define the rights that students do and do not have, including the scope of a student’s right to privacy. The following timeline outlines the history of student privacy with each event labeled as either a limit or extension of student privacy:

1976: Planned Parenthood of Central Mo. V. Danforth

Extension

If a student tells an employee of their school about a pregnancy, that information cannot be disclosed to the student’s parents without consent from the student.

1974: Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA)

Extension

Legislation was passed designed to protect student privacy in regards to educational records. In specific, it allows students “to inspect and review their own education records, request corrections, halt the release of personally identifiable information, and obtain a copy of their institution’s policy concerning access to educational records.”

1974: New Jersey v. T.L.O

Extension with exceptions

Even though school administrators have authority over students, the Fourth Amendment still protect students from unreasonable search and seizure by school officials. However, schools do not need warrants to search a student if they have reason to be suspicious.

1991: Bauer v. Kincaid

Limit

A district court held that criminal investigation reports prepared by campus security are not “education records” and therefore it is not a violation of FERPA for a public university student newspaper to publish them.

1995: Vernonia School Dis. 47J v. Acton

Limit

The Supreme Court held that student athletes could be randomly drug tested, citing a “diminished expectation of privacy” in relation to other students as they are required to pass physical exams before being placed on teams.

2002: United States v. Miami University

Extension

The Sixth Circuit court held that while some disciplinary records are not considered “education records”, a newspaper does not have complete, unrestricted access to student disciplinary records.

2002: Board of Ed. of Independent School Dist. No. 92. Of Pottawatomie Cty. v. Earls

Limit

The Supreme Court ruled that students in extracurricular activities, similar to student athletes, have a “limited expectation of privacy” because of “occasional off-campus travel and communal undress.”

2002: Owasso Independent School District No. I-011 v. Falvo

Limit

The Supreme Court ruled that grades on peer-graded papers were not protected by FERPA.
