Data Collection at UNL

While many college students are probably aware technology companies like Google and Facebook are collecting their data, most are not be aware of the various way in which the university collects student data. In particular, the University of Nebraska-Lincoln (UNL) collects student data both automatically and voluntarily from its students.

Information automatically collected by UNL

According to the UNL online privacy policy, the university automatically collects certain types of information from anyone that browses one of their websites. This anonymous tracking data, known as cookies, is sometimes used in aggregate by the university to observe how users are interacting with their website. The policy further states that UNL uses Google Analytics, an extremely popular web analytics tool used extensively by web developers, as its main means of automatically gathering data from visitors. The information UNL automatically collects does not include personally identifiable information (PII).

Voluntarily collected information

In addition to information automatically collected, the university also collects more personal information, usually some form of PII, with the permission of the user. The UNL online privacy policy states that this information is collected, “to to tailor the content of web pages or other communications to your needs, interests or role(s)”. While the university does collect PII, they explicitly state that this information is only shared to others under the explicit directions of the user. While UNL gives their users the ability to share data with others, they will not do so without the user’s permission.

Tools used by UNL to collect data

The UNL online privacy policy gives some details about what information the university collects, however it is not very specific about how it collects data. In addition to Google Analytics the university states, “Other data-collection and analysis tools may be used at UNL”, as long as no PII is collected. This ambiguity is particularly noticeable when the UNL privacy policy is compared against other college’s online privacy policies. For example, the Stanford online privacy policy not only documents when it collects user data but also explains exactly what data is collected, along with a legal justification for why they are allowed to collect the data. While UNL informs users they use cookies to store data, Stanford lists the names and purposes of every cookie they use.

As universities collect more and more student data, it is important that they keep the user in mind. While today’s privacy policies are a good first step, UNL will need to keep updating this policy as methods of data collection change in the future. By continuing its due diligence, UNL will keep its user aware how their data is being obtained and used.
