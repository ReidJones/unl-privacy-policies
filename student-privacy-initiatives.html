Student Privacy Initiatives

The University of Nebraska, Lincoln is a large educational institution that has been serving the needs of its students since 1869. Since then, UNL has graduated hundreds of thousands of students whose academic records are still stored in university databases. With the sheer quantity of data contained within the university system, UNL administration takes great care to ensure that the information students provide to the university, whether that be academic, financial, or health, is secured and safe from hackers. Over the years, there have been a number of initiatives across the university to protect student data including the removal of social security numbers (SSN) from non-essential functions, providing extensive Virtual Private Network (VPN) access to all students, and a newly implemented dual-factor authentication protocol.

SSN Removal

As outlined on UNL’s SSN and PII website, as of 2007, no university department or office may use a student’s social security number in full or partially as an identifier. Dr. Harvey Pearlman, UNL’s Chancellor at the time, describes that it is the responsibility of UNL administration to keep its students privacy and data safe, so removing SSNs from any application where they are not federally mandated to be included is a logical extension of that responsibility.

In a recent interview, Rick Haugerud, Assistant Vice President of Information Technology Services at UNL, described the processes now in place that ensure all university personnel follow this mandate. Haugerud explained a scanning software that regularly scans all university systems for personally identifiable information (PII) including SNN. UNL also keeps up-to-date records about the offices and systems that have access to PII, and requires these systems to be reviewed annually for PII necessity.

Virtual Private Network

Haugerud also discussed the availability of an extensive VPN for all students. VPNs allow users to have a more secure connection to other networks and can prevent browsing traffic from being exposed over public wi-fi. Providing all students with VPN access adds another layer of security for students when sending sensitive information to various websites. When students complete monetary transactions with online stores or send tax information to the IRS, it is crucial that the information encrypted and on a private network where a passerby wouldn’t be able to intercept it.

Dual-Factor Authentication

One of the most simple methods of security an account is using Dual-Factor or multifactor authentication (2FA or MFA). 2FA adds a layer of security in the form of a physical device in addition to a password. Examples include a text message with a code or an authenticator application.

According to Haugerud, UNL plans to enable 2FA in the Fall of 2019 on its TrueYou system. TrueYou is the University of Nebraska’s single sign on platform. Any student resource provided by UNL uses TrueYou sign on including Canvas, MyRed, and FireFly. The addition of 2FA on all of these systems further protects student data from hacking.
