Software Vendors

Universities across the country differ in many ways, but many have at least one aspect in common: the use of vendor provided software. The term “vendors” here refers specifically to the third-party companies that license their software products to universities and other educational institutions.

Software vendors are everywhere

It's not an exaggeration to say every university in the country uses software from external vendors. According to a 2019 report from the market research company Apps Run the World, the education applications market is projected to be worth $6.9 billion by 2022, a 2.2 percent growth from its 2017 value of $6.2 billion. A different report from the website Inside Higher Ed states that two of the most notable vendors, Canvas and Blackboard, together account for over half of the U.S. higher education learning management system (LMS) market. While the market has many competitors, its clear to see how universities rely on software vendors to provide critical functionality that would otherwise be extremely costly to build in-house.

Vendors at UNL

The University of Nebraska-Lincoln (UNL) is no exception when it comes to using vendors. Steve Booton, the university registrar for UNL, said in an interview that the university has contracts with many software vendors that provide products for a plethora of niche jobs. In addition to using Canvas for its LMS, UNL uses academic advising software from Hobson Starfish, record imaging software from Perceptive Content, and admissions software from Talisma, just to name a few examples.

Protection of student data from vendors

The use of third-party software vendors raises one question in particular concerning student privacy; how is student data protected when it is being shared with companies outside the university? Students cannot opt out of critical software like Canvas, and other software from vendors, like Degree Audit and True You, are so seamlessly incorporated in the university’s website that some students might not even know they are using vendor-provided software. Additionally, UNL prefers to store student data on the vendor’s servers instead of granting vendors access to the entire university network. This creates the possibility that a student can send their data to a server outside of the university.

To protect student data, UNL requires all vendors to sign a contract with the university. Each contract is tailored to address the needs of a particular vendor, but they all state that student data is property of the university and cannot be used by the vendor for any reason other than those specified in the contract. In the same interview Steve Booton mentioned that UNL is looking to extend its protection of student data by requiring vendors to erase student data after a period of time.
